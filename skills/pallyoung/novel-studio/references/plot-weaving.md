# Plot Weaving

## 1. Goal

Design a story that does not move as a single flat line. Use main plot, side plots, relationship lines, suspense lines, and pressure lines so the work gains density, emotional variation, and long-form momentum.

This reference is for:
- weaving main plot and side plots together
- deciding when to insert, escalate, pause, or recover a subplot
- preventing side plots from becoming decorative filler
- ensuring multi-line structure still serves one readable whole

---

## 2. Core principle

A side plot is not a side dish. It is a pressure system for the main plot.

A valid side plot should do at least one of the following:
- increase the cost of the main goal
- expose a different side of the protagonist
- create new constraints on the protagonist's choices
- create emotional turbulence that changes later decisions
- carry suspense, secrets, or delayed payoff back into the main plot
- reshape pacing so the story does not move in one emotional register

If a side plot does none of these, it is likely filler.

---

## 3. Plot-line types

### 3.1 Main plot line
The dominant objective and conflict engine of the story.
Examples:
- revenge
- survival
- power rise
- escape
- solving a mystery
- defending a city, family, faction, or relationship

### 3.2 Relationship line
The emotional or relational engine between key characters.
Examples:
- romance
- rivalry
- master-disciple tension
- family fracture
- friendship under pressure
- loyalty vs betrayal

### 3.3 Character-growth line
The protagonist or a major character changing through choices, losses, illusions breaking, or values being rebuilt.

### 3.4 Suspense / secret line
A hidden truth, delayed reveal, mistaken identity, old case, prophecy, conspiracy, hidden bloodline, missing memory, etc.

### 3.5 Pressure line
An external force that keeps intensifying:
- countdown
- political persecution
- approaching war
- debt
- curse
- sickness
- sect pressure
- public reputation collapse

---

## 4. Minimum line-combination rule

For long-form web fiction, prefer at least:
- 1 main plot line
- 1 relationship line
- 1 suspense or pressure line

A story with only one clear line often feels thin, linear, and predictable.

---

## 5. Side-plot design checklist

For every major side plot, define:
- what this side plot is about
- which characters it belongs to
- what emotional color it adds
- what pressure it adds to the main plot
- what decision it changes
- where it peaks
- where it feeds back into the main plot
- where it closes or transforms

If you cannot answer these, the side plot is underdesigned.

---

## 6. Interweaving patterns

### 6.1 Pressure insertion
Use a side plot to raise stakes when the main plot becomes too straightforward.
Example:
- the protagonist can win, but the romance line makes the win morally expensive
- the protagonist can leave, but family obligation keeps pulling him back

### 6.2 Character exposure insertion
Use a side plot to reveal who someone is under emotional, social, or moral pressure.
This is especially strong for:
- love line
- family line
- subordinate/superior line
- rival line

### 6.3 Suspense relay
Let the main plot pause in visible action while the suspense line takes over tension.
This prevents the middle from sagging.

### 6.4 Emotional contrast insertion
After a high-pressure main-plot sequence, insert a side plot that shifts emotional temperature without killing momentum.
This can be:
- intimacy before betrayal
- temporary peace before disaster
- comedy before collapse
- tenderness before cruelty

### 6.5 Delayed recoil
A side plot seems secondary early, but later returns and changes the result of the main plot.
This is one of the best ways to make the story feel “woven” instead of segmented.

---

## 7. When to insert side plots

Use side plots when:
- the main plot has been escalating in one register for too long
- a relationship needs to alter later choices
- the protagonist feels efficient but not emotionally alive
- the story needs suspense beyond direct confrontation
- the middle portion lacks freshness or emotional variation
- a later payoff needs earlier planting

Do not insert side plots only because “this section feels empty.”
A side plot inserted just to fill space usually weakens the book.

---

## 8. Recovery and payoff rule

A side plot must eventually do at least one of the following:
- change a major decision
- change a relationship state
- reveal a truth that redefines the main plot
- increase the cost of victory
- produce a betrayal, rescue, alliance, or sacrifice
- turn emotional energy into plot consequence

If a side plot never feeds back into consequence, it remains decorative.

---

## 9. Main/subplot connection tests

Before approving an outline, ask for each important side plot:
- If this line is removed, what does the main story lose?
- What cost, pressure, or revelation does this line add?
- Which later scene becomes stronger because this line exists?
- Does this line change the protagonist's choices or only accompany them?

If the answer is weak, strengthen or cut the line.

---

## 10. Common failure patterns

### 10.1 Decorative side plot
Exists for atmosphere but changes nothing.

### 10.2 Isolated romance line
The relationship exists, but never changes timing, loyalty, risk, or sacrifice.

### 10.3 Detached backstory line
A tragic past is explained, but never becomes living pressure in present action.

### 10.4 Endless branch sprawl
Too many branches open, but none close or converge.

### 10.5 Mid-story line collapse
The side plot starts promisingly, then disappears without payoff.

---

## 11. Practical workflow use

During outlining:
- map every important line
- note where each line enters, intensifies, and pays off
- ensure the middle section is carried by more than one tension source

During drafting:
- check whether the current chapter advances only the main objective or also one other line
- look for chances to let relational, emotional, or suspense pressure alter decisions

During proofreading:
- check whether side plots actually changed consequence
- flag any line that exists but does not transform later scenes
