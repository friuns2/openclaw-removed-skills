# Character Craft

## 1. Goal

Create characters who are not just functional labels, but people with presence, contradiction, attraction, and scene power.

This reference is for:
- making protagonists memorable
- preventing supporting cast from becoming tools
- using language, action, environment, and other-character reactions to shape character
- designing attraction, contradiction, weakness, and pressure

---

## 2. Core principle

A strong character is not built from labels. A strong character is built from:
- desire
- wound
- contradiction
- cost
- distinctive behavior
- relationship pressure

“Cold,” “smart,” “gentle,” “crazy,” and “strong” are not enough on their own.
Those are tags, not dramatic life.

---

## 3. Six main tools of characterization

### 3.1 Action
What a character does under pressure tells more truth than profile text.
Use action to show:
- priorities
- fear
- cruelty
- protectiveness
- arrogance
- restraint
- obsession

### 3.2 Speech
Speech shows class, rhythm, distance, confidence, education, emotional control, and hidden desire.
Define:
- sentence length tendency
- directness vs indirection
- sarcasm vs sincerity
- formal vs colloquial tone
- what the character avoids saying

### 3.3 Reaction under stress
A memorable character reacts in a recognizable way when cornered.
Examples:
- becomes colder
- becomes reckless
- jokes harder
- gets meticulous
- lashes out at the wrong target
- protects someone instead of themself

### 3.4 Environment and belongings
A room, desk, weapon, clothing habit, phone wallpaper, handwriting style, food preference, or ritual can reveal character indirectly.
Use environment to signal:
- discipline vs chaos
- loneliness vs attachment
- vanity vs neglect
- control vs decay

### 3.5 Other-character perspective
A character becomes stronger when the world has an opinion about them.
Use others to show:
- fear
- misunderstanding
- admiration
- resentment
- dependence
- rumors
- myth vs reality contrast

### 3.6 Contrast and contradiction
A memorable character often holds two forces at once.
Examples:
- ruthless but loyal
- elegant but vicious
- arrogant but deeply ashamed
- emotionally detached but obsessively protective
- naïve in one domain and terrifying in another

---

## 4. Protagonist attraction structure

A protagonist should usually define at least:
- surface identity
- core desire
- core wound
- inner contradiction
- capability and cost
- what makes the protagonist hard to replace

### Surface identity
Who the protagonist appears to be.

### Core desire
What the protagonist most wants, not just what the plot says they need.

### Core wound
What past damage still governs choices.

### Inner contradiction
The most attractive conflict inside the protagonist.
This often creates charm.

### Capability and cost
Power without cost becomes flat wish-fulfillment.
Cost creates tension and respect.

### Irreplaceability
What does this protagonist do or feel in a way that no generic hero would?

---

## 5. Supporting-cast design rule

Every core supporting character should have:
- an independent goal
- an independent value system
- a distinct tension with the protagonist
- a memorable hook

A memorable hook can come from:
- a dangerous calmness
- a cruel tenderness
- a ritual habit
- a specific moral line
- a self-destructive loyalty
- a sharp verbal style
- a striking contradiction

---

## 6. Character through scene, not only profile

Do not rely only on bios.
A character becomes real when scenes reveal:
- what they hide
- what embarrasses them
- what they refuse to lose
- what makes them cruel
- what makes them soft
- what line they will not cross
- what line they say they will not cross but eventually do

---

## 7. Choosing the right technique

### For first appearance
Prefer:
- action
- other-character reaction
- one vivid detail
- one immediate pressure response

Avoid opening with long profile exposition.

### For building charm
Prefer:
- difficult choices
- visible cost
- contradiction
- a distinct way of speaking or acting

### For deepening complexity
Prefer:
- moments of weakness
- hidden habits
- shame triggers
- value conflict
- relational tension

### For showing status or aura
Prefer:
- how others behave around them
- how space changes when they enter
- what they do not need to say

---

## 8. Common weak-character patterns

### 8.1 Label stack
The file says a lot, but scenes do not prove it.

### 8.2 Functional puppet
The character exists only to explain, assist, obstruct, or love the protagonist.

### 8.3 No independent agenda
Without the protagonist, the character has no life force.

### 8.4 Same-voice cast
Everyone speaks with the same rhythm and emotional logic.

### 8.5 No contradiction
The character is exactly what the summary says, nothing more.

---

## 9. Practical workflow use

During character design:
- define attraction source, contradiction, cost, and relationship pressure

During outlining:
- check whether key scenes force characters to reveal those traits

During drafting:
- use scene behavior, dialogue rhythm, and other-character reactions to keep them alive

During proofreading:
- ask whether each core character could be swapped out without much loss
If yes, the character is still underdeveloped.
