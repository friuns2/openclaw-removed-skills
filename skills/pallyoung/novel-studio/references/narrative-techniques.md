# Narrative Techniques

## 1. Goal

Use narrative techniques consciously so pacing, revelation, emotional pressure, and payoff feel designed rather than accidental.

This reference is for:
- choosing the right dramatic pattern for a scene, chapter, or arc
- understanding when to use suppression-and-release, misdirection, reversal, information gaps, and contrast
- making story motion feel shaped, not flat

---

## 2. Core principle

A technique is not a decoration. It is a way of controlling reader expectation, emotional timing, and pressure.

Choose techniques based on the target effect:
- awe
- suspense
- pity
- rage
- catharsis
- tenderness
- dread
- triumph

---

## 3. Common narrative patterns

### 3.1 先抑后扬 (suppress then raise)
Use when:
- you want to build underestimation before payoff
- the protagonist needs a stronger rise in aura
- humiliation, dismissal, or concealment should amplify later reversal

Best for:
- face-slapping scenes
- hidden-power reveals
- underdog rise

Risk:
If suppression is too long or too weakly paid off, it becomes frustrating instead of satisfying.

### 3.2 先扬后抑 (raise then suppress)
Use when:
- you want emotional shock, tragedy, betrayal, or collapse
- early joy or hope should make later damage heavier

Best for:
- relationship heartbreak
- false victory collapse
- paradise-before-ruin structures

Risk:
If the “raise” is not emotionally convincing, the “fall” feels manipulative.

### 3.3 欲扬先抑
A more focused variant where a character, relationship, or truth is first obscured, doubted, or dismissed, then elevated.
Strong for character charm and reader reevaluation.

### 3.4 Misdirection + reversal
Use when:
- you want the reader to think they understand what is happening, then discover a more dangerous truth

Best for:
- suspense
- conspiracy
- identity twists
- strategic mind games

### 3.5 Information-gap propulsion
Use when:
- the reader should keep reading to close a gap in knowledge
- mystery or emotional uncertainty carries momentum

### 3.6 Countdown pressure
Use when:
- pacing needs urgency
- action must accelerate toward a deadline

### 3.7 Double-line advance
Advance two lines at once:
- plot line + relationship line
- action line + suspense line
- external pressure + internal collapse

This is powerful against flatness.

---

## 4. Choosing techniques by story need

### To build protagonist charm
Prefer:
- suppress then raise
- delayed reveal
- controlled competence reveal
- contrast between public misunderstanding and private reality

### To intensify emotional investment
Prefer:
- raise then suppress
- delayed confession
- interrupted intimacy
- emotional near-miss

### To fight middle-section collapse
Prefer:
- double-line advance
- information-gap propulsion
- delayed recoil from earlier side plots
- pressure line escalation

### To create strong chapter endings
Prefer:
- reversal
- newly opened cost
- withheld truth
- impossible choice
- emotional rupture

---

## 5. Technique misuse warnings

### 5.1 Fake reversal
The twist exists, but changes no emotional or plot consequence.

### 5.2 Endless suppression without release
Creates reader fatigue.

### 5.3 Repeated same-shape payoff
If every chapter uses the same trick, the book becomes predictable.

### 5.4 Information withholding without fairness
If the reader feels cheated rather than surprised, trust drops.

---

## 6. Practical workflow use

During outlining:
- map major technique choices at arc level
- decide where suppression, release, reversal, and emotional collapse happen

During chapter planning:
- decide each chapter's primary narrative engine
- avoid making every chapter rely on the same engine

During drafting:
- execute the chosen pattern cleanly
- keep setup and payoff proportionate

During proofreading:
- check whether the intended emotional effect actually lands
- flag false tension, fake twists, or stale repeated technique patterns
