# Language and Rhetoric

## 1. Goal

Use rhetoric and style as tools for effect, not decoration for its own sake.

This reference is for:
- choosing language intensity by scene purpose
- deciding when to use metaphor, repetition, parallelism, contrast, irony, or restraint
- matching style to genre, pacing, and emotional target
- avoiding fake-literary over-decoration

---

## 2. Core principle

Style must serve reading effect.
Do not use literary devices just because they sound “pretty.”
Use them because they sharpen:
- image
- rhythm
- emotion
- tension
- atmosphere
- character voice

If a device weakens pace, clarity, or impact, cut it.

---

## 3. Scene-first language choice

Before choosing rhetoric, ask:
- Is this scene for speed, emotion, suspense, aura, tenderness, cruelty, or revelation?
- Does the scene need compression or expansion?
- Should the prose feel sharp, lush, cold, intimate, explosive, or restrained?

Choose rhetoric after the function is clear.

---

## 4. Major devices and when to use them

### 4.1 Metaphor / simile
Use when:
- the scene needs stronger image or emotional transfer
- the feeling is difficult to state directly
- one sharp comparison can replace three flat sentences

Do not overuse in high-speed action or high-clarity exposition.
Too many metaphors slow momentum.

### 4.2 Repetition
Use when:
- obsession, grief, insistence, humiliation, or rising pressure needs emphasis
- a line needs ritual or incantatory force

Too much repetition can feel melodramatic if not emotionally earned.

### 4.3 Parallelism / rhythmic listing
Use when:
- building momentum
- delivering high-emotion declarations
- creating strong rhetorical lift

Works better in climactic or emotionally charged moments than in neutral exposition.

### 4.4 Contrast / antithesis
Use when:
- highlighting contradiction
- showing inner split
- intensifying irony
- creating strong before/after or mask/true-self effect

### 4.5 Irony
Use when:
- the narration or context should expose a gap between belief and truth
- the emotional effect benefits from cool distance or bitter intelligence

### 4.6 Scenic projection
Use when:
- environment should carry emotional color
- the pacing allows reflective texture

Avoid replacing actual dramatic movement with decorative scenery.

---

## 5. Matching style to genre and target effect

### Fast web-novel action /爽感 scenes
Prefer:
- short to medium sentences
- sharp verbs
- concrete payoff
- clean readability
- limited but strong rhetorical bursts

### Intimate emotional scenes
Prefer:
- sensory detail
- pauses
- subtext
- restraint
- selective metaphor

### Suspense / mystery scenes
Prefer:
- controlled information
- precision
- calm surface with hidden pressure
- fewer decorative flourishes

### Grand / epic / ancient-style scenes
Prefer:
- stronger cadence
- more formal balance
- occasional parallel structure or elevated diction

But still protect readability.

---

## 6. Misuse warnings

### 6.1 Dense metaphor stacking
Creates fog, not depth.

### 6.2 Pretty but empty lines
They sound literary, but add no image, tension, or emotional truth.

### 6.3 Too much balanced phrasing
Can make prose feel artificial, performative, or old-fashioned in the wrong genre.

### 6.4 Abstract emotion without concrete support
Emotion becomes vague when not anchored in body, action, object, or choice.

---

## 7. Practical workflow use

During drafting:
- choose rhetoric based on scene function
- use stronger devices sparingly at emotional peaks
- keep exposition and transitional scenes cleaner

During polishing:
- cut decorative excess
- sharpen images
- replace generic emotional statements with specific language or action

During proofreading:
- check whether rhetoric supports rather than smothers the reading experience
