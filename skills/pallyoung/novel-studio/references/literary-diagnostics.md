# Literary Diagnostics

## 1. Goal

Diagnose why a story, outline, chapter, or character feels weak before attempting revision.

This reference is for:
- identifying whether a problem is structural, character-based, tonal, rhetorical, or pacing-related
- choosing the right fix rather than generic rewriting
- turning “something feels off” into a usable diagnosis

---

## 2. Core principle

Do not revise blindly.
Diagnose the dominant problem first.
A weak chapter can fail for very different reasons:
- no real conflict
- no emotional shift
- no information gain
- no hook
- weak character pressure
- wrong language density
- dead side plot

The fix depends on the cause.

A diagnosis is not valid if it stays at the level of vague feeling only.
Do not say “it feels weak” without naming the mechanism.
If the evidence is insufficient, request more material or context instead of pretending certainty.

---

## 3. Diagnostic families

### 3.1 Plot diagnosis
Check:
- Is the main objective clear?
- Is conflict escalating?
- Does the chapter change consequence?
- Do side plots feed back into the main line?
- Are there visible payoff paths?

### 3.2 Character diagnosis
Check:
- Does the protagonist have attraction beyond labels?
- Do key characters have contradiction, desire, and cost?
- Could this character be replaced without much loss?
- Does speech differ between characters?
- Do scenes reveal character through choice and reaction?

### 3.3 Chapter diagnosis
Check:
- What changes in this chapter?
- Is there a conflict, reversal, reveal, relationship shift, or cost?
- Does the ending create forward pull?
- Is the chapter only transition and explanation?

### 3.4 Language diagnosis
Check:
- Is the prose too flat?
- Is the prose too decorative?
- Are emotions abstract rather than embodied?
- Does language match scene function?

### 3.5 Subplot diagnosis
Check:
- Does this line change the protagonist's decisions?
- Does it add pressure, suspense, or emotional consequence?
- Does it pay off later?
- If removed, what really changes?

---

## 4. Common symptom → likely cause

### Symptom: “The plot feels flat.”
Likely causes:
- only one tension source
- no side-line pressure
- weak reversal structure
- no cost escalation

### Symptom: “The character feels thin.”
Likely causes:
- label-only design
- no contradiction
- no independent desire
- no cost or weak reaction scenes

### Symptom: “The chapter has things happening but still feels dull.”
Likely causes:
- events happen, but no emotional or relational change
- scene purpose is unclear
- no hook or unresolved pressure at the end

### Symptom: “The prose feels fancy but empty.”
Likely causes:
- rhetorical density too high
- weak image support
- abstract language replacing actual dramatic movement

### Symptom: “The side plot feels useless.”
Likely causes:
- no feedback into the main plot
- no consequence path
- decorative rather than functional design

---

## 5. Revision decision rule

After diagnosis, name:
- the primary problem
- the secondary problem
- the smallest effective fix
- the deeper structural fix if the small fix is not enough

Do not give generic feedback like:
- “more vivid”
- “more emotional”
- “more detailed”
without naming what mechanism is missing.

---

## 6. Practical workflow use

During outlining review:
- diagnose line structure, pressure diversity, and payoff design

During character review:
- diagnose attraction, differentiation, and relationship force

During chapter review:
- diagnose conflict movement, emotional movement, and hook effectiveness

During proofreading:
- identify the dominant failure mode before suggesting revisions
