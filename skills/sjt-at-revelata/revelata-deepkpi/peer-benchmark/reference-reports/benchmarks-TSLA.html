Diff Insight
What the benchmark set reveals

On automotive, GM and Ford provide scale context but no meaningful margin comp. Ford's Model e ran at −131.8% EBIT margin (FY2024) while Tesla's automotive gross margin was 17.8% — a ~150pp structural spread that reflects the difference between a direct-sales software-defined EV platform and a dealer-distributed ICE company running an EV experiment. The absence of any pure-play EV OEM at scale in the S&P 1500 means this benchmark set can only bound the analysis from below, not match it.

The energy segment diff reveals a coverage void that grows more significant each year. Tesla's 46.7 GWh deployed at $12.8B revenue is already larger than any standalone storage deployment company — Enphase covers residential at 0.7% of TSLA's GWh, AES covers utility-scale generation but as a project developer with incomparable economics. The FSD/Supercharging layer compounds this: a high-margin software-defined autonomous driving platform with global Supercharger monetization has no listed pure-play comparable anywhere in the S&P 1500.

Notes
  • No pure EV comp at scale: No EV-native OEM approaches TSLA's 1.64M delivery volume in the S&P 1500. GM and Ford are volume comps only, not margin comps.
  • Metric mismatch — GM and Ford: TSLA reports gross margin; GM and Ford report adjusted EBIT. EBIT is post-SG&A; gross margin is pre. The true margin gap is larger than the raw numbers suggest.
  • Auto margin compression: TSLA automotive GM fell from 29.3% (FY2021) to 17.8% (FY2025) from price cuts and capacity ramp — still structurally exceeds any traditional OEM's auto segment margin.
  • FSD/Robotaxi/Supercharging coverage gap: Tesla's growing high-margin software revenue is embedded in Automotive "Services and Other." No listed company monetizes an autonomous driving platform + charging network at comparable scale.
